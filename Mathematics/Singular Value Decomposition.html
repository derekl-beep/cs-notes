Singular Value Decomposition (SVD)

SVD is:

  • Data reduction
  • Data-driven generalisation of Fourier transform (FFT)
  • “Tailored” to specific problems
  • Simple and interpretable linear algebra

SVD can solve:

  • Ax=bAx=bAx=b, for non-squared AAA regression
  • basis PCA
  • correlations

Real-world applications include:

  • Google’s PageRank
  • Facebook’s facial recognition
  • Netflix’s recommendation system

The SVD of a real matrix is guaranteed to exist, and to be unique.

To compute the SVD in MATLAB, we can do

Dominant Correlations

The UUU and VVV are the eigenvectors of the correlation matrices XXTXX^TXXT and XTXX^TXXTX respectively.

Given X∈Rn×mX \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times m}X∈Rn×m,

XTX=[x1Tx1x1Tx2…x1Txmx2Tx1x2Tx2…x2Txm⋮⋮⋱⋮xmTx1xmTx2…xmTxm]m×m X^{T}X = \begin{bmatrix} x_1^T x_1 & x_1^T x_2 & \dots & x_1^T x_m \\ x_2^T x_1 & x_2^T x_2 & \dots & x_2^T x_m \\ \vdots & \vdots &\ddots & \vdots \\ x_m^T x_1 & x_m^T x_2 & \dots & x_m^T x_m \end{bmatrix}_{m \times m} XTX=⎣⎢⎢⎢⎡​x1T​x1​x2T​x1​⋮xmT​x1​​x1T​x2​x2T​x2​⋮xmT​x2​​……⋱…​x1T​xm​x2T​xm​⋮xmT​xm​​⎦⎥⎥⎥⎤​m×m​

is the correlation matrix among the columns of XXX. Each non-diagonal entry of the matrix is an inner product between two columns, i.e. xiTxj=⟨xi,xj⟩x_i^T x_j = \langle x_i, x_j \ranglexiT​xj​=⟨xi​,xj​⟩. Each diagonal entry is an inner product of the column itself. Hence, the matrix contains information of the linear correlation between columns.

For example, if each column represents the flatten pixels of an individual face image, a large inner product tells us that the two faces are similar, having the same basic face structure, and vice versa.

Note that the correlation matrix XTXX^T XXTX is symmetric and positive semi-definite. The eigenvalues of the matrix are guaranteed to be non-negative, i.e. λi≥0,∀I\lambda_i \geq 0, \forall Iλi​≥0,∀I.

Given X=U^Σ^VTX = \hat{U}\hat{\Sigma}V^TX=U^Σ^VT,

XTX=(VΣ^TU^T)(U^Σ^VT)=VΣ^2VT. X^T X = (V\hat{\Sigma}^T \hat{U}^T)(\hat{U}\hat{\Sigma}V^T)=V \hat{\Sigma}^2 V^T. XTX=(VΣ^TU^T)(U^Σ^VT)=VΣ^2VT.
Multiplying both side by VVV gives
XTXV=VΣ^2. X^T X V = V \hat{\Sigma}^2. XTXV=VΣ^2.

Thus, VVV, the right singular vectors, and Σ^2\hat{\Sigma}^2Σ^2, the squares of (economy) singular values, are the eigenvectors and the eigenvalues of the column-wise correlation matrix XTXX^T XXTX respectively.

Simliarly, U^\hat{U}U^, the (economy) left singular vectors, and Σ^2\hat{\Sigma}^2Σ^2, the squares of the (economy) singular values, are the eigenvectors and the eigenvalues of the row-wise correlation matrix XXTX X^TXXT respectively.

XXT=(U^Σ^VT)(VΣ^TU^T)=U^Σ^2U^T X X^T = (\hat{U}\hat{\Sigma}V^T)(V\hat{\Sigma}^T \hat{U}^T) =\hat{U} \hat{\Sigma}^2 \hat{U}^T XXT=(U^Σ^VT)(VΣ^TU^T)=U^Σ^2U^T
Multiplying both side by U^\hat{U}U^ gives
XTXU^=U^Σ^2. X^T X \hat{U} = \hat{U} \hat{\Sigma}^2. XTXU^=U^Σ^2.

Generally, U^\hat{U}U^, Σ^\hat{\Sigma}Σ^ and VVV are not computed using this concept because of the inefficiency of eigendecomposition algorithms. More efficient methods include those based on QR factorisation.
