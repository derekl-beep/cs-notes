Linear Systems

Linear systems of ordinary differential equations (ODEs)

x˙=Ax \dot{x}=Ax x˙=Ax

, where x∈Rnx \in \mathbb{R}^nx∈Rn is the state vector,
and A∈Rn×nA \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}A∈Rn×n is the system matrix.

The basic solution is

x(t)=eAtx(0) x(t) = e^{At}x(0) x(t)=eAtx(0)

, where eAt=I+tA+t22!A2+t33!A3+...e^{At}=I+tA+\frac{t^2}{2!}A^2+\frac{t^3}{3!}A^3+...eAt=I+tA+2!t2​A2+3!t3​A3+....
However, computing this infinite sum of matrices are numerically difficult.

Eigenvalues and Eigenvectors of the System Matrix

Aξ=λξA \xi = \lambda \xiAξ=λξ

Any vector ξi∈Rn≠0\xi_i \in \mathbb{R}^n \neq 0ξi​∈Rn​=0 and constant λi∈C\lambda_i \in \mathbb{C}λi​∈C satisfying this equation are the eigenvector and the eigenvalue of matrix AAA respectively.

Tackling dynamical systems in the eigenvector coordinates are advantageous. It gives us diagonal system dynamics, which is easier to solve.

Closed-form expression of eAte^{At}eAt

Given A=TDT−1A=TDT^{-1}A=TDT−1, the computation of eAte^{At}eAt can be simplified by the use of the eigenvector and eigenvalue matrices as eAt=TeDtT−1e^{At}=T e^{Dt} T^{-1}eAt=TeDtT−1, and hence, x(t)=TeDtT−1x(0)x(t)=T e^{Dt} T^{-1}x(0)x(t)=TeDtT−1x(0).

Stability and Eigenvalues

Stability tells us what the states of the systems will be as time goes to infinity.

Continuous systems

The basic solution given the initial condition is x(t)=TeDtT−1x(0)x(t)=Te^{Dt}T^{-1}x(0)x(t)=TeDtT−1x(0).

The stability of the system only depends on the time-varying quantities, i.e. eDte^{Dt}eDt.

eDt=[eλ1t0…00eλ2t…0⋮⋮⋱⋮00…eλnt]e^{Dt}=\begin{bmatrix} e^{\lambda_1 t} & 0 & \dots & 0 \\ 0 & e^{\lambda_2 t} & \dots & 0 \\ \vdots & \vdots &\ddots & \vdots \\ 0 & 0 & \dots & e^{\lambda_n t} \end{bmatrix}eDt=⎣⎢⎢⎢⎡​eλ1​t0⋮0​0eλ2​t⋮0​……⋱…​00⋮eλn​t​⎦⎥⎥⎥⎤​

Given λ=a+ib∈C\lambda = a + ib \in \mathbb{C}λ=a+ib∈C, eλt=eat[cos⁡(bt)+isin⁡(bt)]e^{\lambda t}=e^{at}[\cos{(bt)}+i\sin{(bt)}]eλt=eat[cos(bt)+isin(bt)]. The amplitude of the sinusoidal terms is always within unity. The exponential term (the real part of the eigenvalue), hence, determines the stability behaviour of the system.

Source: https://youtu.be/h7nJ6ZL4Lf0?list=PLMrJAkhIeNNR20Mz-VpzgfQs5zrYi085m

The system is stable if and only if all the real part of the eigenvalues of the system matrix are non-positive. Re(λi)≤0,∀i=1,2,...,n\text{Re}(\lambda_i) \leq0, \forall i = 1,2,...,nRe(λi​)≤0,∀i=1,2,...,n

To control an unstable system with feedbacks, e.g. u=−Kxu=-Kxu=−Kx, the system behaviours, as well as the eigenvalues, can be changed.

x˙=Ax+Bu=Ax−BKx=(A−BK)x\dot{x}=Ax+Bu=Ax-BKx=(A-BK)xx˙=Ax+Bu=Ax−BKx=(A−BK)x

xk+1=A~xkx_{k+1}=\tilde{A}x_kxk+1​=A~xk​, where xk=x(kΔt)x_k=x(k\Delta t)xk​=x(kΔt), and A~=eAΔt\tilde{A}=e^{A \Delta t}A~=eAΔt.

Linearizing Around a Fixed Point

The non-linear dynamics of a system of ODEs can be described as x˙=f(x)\dot{x}=f(x)x˙=f(x), where x∈Rnx \in \mathbb{R}^nx∈Rn is the state vector.

Linearization steps

Step 1: find the fixed points xˉ\bar{x}xˉ s.t. f(xˉ)=0f(\bar{x})=0f(xˉ)=0
Step 2: Linearize fff about xˉ\bar{x}xˉ using the Jocabian matrix DfDx∣xˉ=[∂fi∂xj]\frac{Df}{Dx}|_{\bar{x}}=[\frac{\partial f_i}{\partial x_j}]DxDf​∣xˉ​=[∂xj​∂fi​​]

Example

Given
x1˙=f1(x1,x2)=x1x2x2˙=f2(x1,x2)=x12+x22 \begin{aligned} \dot{x_1} &= f_1(x_1, x_2) = x_1x_2 \\ \dot{x_2} &= f_2(x_1, x_2) = x_1^2+x_2^2 \end{aligned} x1​˙​x2​˙​​=f1​(x1​,x2​)=x1​x2​=f2​(x1​,x2​)=x12​+x22​​, we have
DfDx=[∂f1∂x1∂f1∂x2∂f2∂x1∂f2∂x2]=[x2x12x12x2] \frac{Df}{Dx} =\begin{bmatrix} \frac{\partial f_1}{\partial x_1} & \frac{\partial f_1}{\partial x_2} \\ \frac{\partial f_2}{\partial x_1} & \frac{\partial f_2}{\partial x_2} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} x_2 & x_1 \\ 2x_1 & 2x_2 \end{bmatrix} DxDf​=[∂x1​∂f1​​∂x1​∂f2​​​∂x2​∂f1​​∂x2​∂f2​​​]=[x2​2x1​​x1​2x2​​], and hence
{x1˙x2˙}≈[xˉ2xˉ12xˉ12xˉ2]{x1x2} \begin{Bmatrix} \dot{x_1} \\ \dot{x_2} \end{Bmatrix} \approx \begin{bmatrix} \bar{x}_2 & \bar{x}_1 \\ 2\bar{x}_1 & 2\bar{x}_2 \end{bmatrix} \begin{Bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \end{Bmatrix} {x1​˙​x2​˙​​}≈[xˉ2​2xˉ1​​xˉ1​2xˉ2​​]{x1​x2​​}.

Proof

By the Taylor expression,

x˙=f(x)=f(xˉ)+DfDx∣xˉ⋅(x−xˉ)+D2fDx2∣xˉ⋅(x−xˉ)2+…≈f(xˉ)+DfDx∣xˉ⋅(x−xˉ) \begin{aligned} \dot{x}=f(x)&=f(\bar{x})+\frac{Df}{Dx}|_{\bar{x}} \cdot (x-\bar{x}) + \frac{D^2f}{Dx^2}|_{\bar{x}} \cdot (x-\bar{x})^2 + \dots \\ &\approx f(\bar{x})+\frac{Df}{Dx}|_{\bar{x}} \cdot (x-\bar{x}) \end{aligned} x˙=f(x)​=f(xˉ)+DxDf​∣xˉ​⋅(x−xˉ)+Dx2D2f​∣xˉ​⋅(x−xˉ)2+…≈f(xˉ)+DxDf​∣xˉ​⋅(x−xˉ)​

By definition, we choose xˉ\bar{x}xˉ s.t. f(xˉ)=0f(\bar{x})=0f(xˉ)=0, so
Δx˙=DfDx∣xˉΔx=AΔx\Delta \dot{x} = \frac{Df}{Dx}|_{\bar{x}} \Delta x = A \Delta xΔx˙=DxDf​∣xˉ​Δx=AΔx

Remark

The approximation is only true around the vincinity of xˉ\bar{x}xˉ, where the system is instantaneous stable, by adding feedback control, e.g. u=−Kxu=-Kxu=−Kx, we aim to stablize the system around xˉ\bar{x}xˉ. Therefore, A=DfDx∣xˉA=\frac{Df}{Dx}|_{\bar{x}}A=DxDf​∣xˉ​ can be considered as time-invariant, and is a good linear approximation of f(x)f(x)f(x) within the space we’re interested in.
